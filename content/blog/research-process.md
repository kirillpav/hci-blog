## Research Process
Our project is an AI-powered road trip assistant built specifically for EV drivers. They are a compelling group to research because, in addition to standard road-trip struggles, they face the added challenge of managing battery life and locating chargers. Furthermore, EVs are more susceptible to unforeseen variables, making a dedicated assistant essential for a smooth trip.

Going into the research phase, I initially assumed EV drivers only worried about where to charge. However, as we dove deeper, we discovered a myriad of other challenges that travelers face. From calculating the impact of elevation on battery range to navigating unreliable charging hardware, the scope of the problem is much broader than we first realized.

Our primary research method was interviews. We were fortunate to conduct an insightful in-class interview with an EV owner, and another teammate who owns an EV shared crucial firsthand experiences that helped us build our initial personas. To round out our qualitative data, we conducted a third interview with another EV driver. We also supplemented these findings by browsing subreddits and online communities dedicated to travel and electric vehicles, where we noted the most common recurring concerns from the broader EV community.

To organize our research, we began with Affinity Diagramming. We captured quick, useful insights from our interviews and online research into a broad collection of findings. While this initial phase was intentionally high-level and fast-paced, it allowed us to iterate quickly and see the "big picture" of the EV driver’s journey.

![Affinity Diagram](/imgs/blog2/AffinityDiagram.png)

From there, we transitioned to Identity Themes. We identified five core categories:
Technical Issues
Discussion
Interaction
Group Learning
Individual Learning
By mapping our affinity notes to these themes, we were able to streamline our findings and clearly outline the pain points that would define our solution.

## Persona Development
Next, we generated a High-Level Persona to represent our ideal user. Our primary focus was someone who is highly tech-savvy but also an avid traveler. This led us to Leo, the “Optimizing Explorer.” Leo works in the tech industry and drives a Tesla Model 3, representing a user who values efficiency and data-driven travel.
To dive deeper into Leo’s mindset, we developed an Empathy Map covering four primary quadrants:
Hears
Sees
Says & Does
Thinks & Feels
For each category, we brainstormed specific points based directly on our research findings. Finally, we mapped out Leo's Pains and Gains, specifically identifying the hurdles he currently faces and the value our application will provide to overcome them.
Following our persona development, we created an Experience Map to visualize the end-to-end journey of an EV driver. We mapped user actions, goals, emotions, and thoughts across five distinct stages of a trip. By identifying specific pain points and opportunities at each stage, we were able to further refine our research and clearly visualize exactly how a potential user would interact with our app in a real-world scenario.

## My Learnings
Perhaps my most significant learning was realizing how many hidden pain points EV drivers navigate. In essence, their entire journey is dictated by charging logistics, which severely limits their ability to explore or be spontaneous. The true 'wonder' of a road trip is the freedom to see something on a whim, but for an EV driver, that freedom is often traded for the unpredictability of vehicle range and the 'unknowns' of charging availability.
The most valuable insight we gained is that travelers crave the freedom to explore without being tied to charging logistics and the granular details of the trip. Our app is the ideal solution to this gap. By leveraging AI-driven navigation, we can offload the cognitive load of charging and planning to the assistant. This allows users to reclaim the spontaneity of the road and actually enjoy their journey, rather than just managing it.
Our research allowed us to define clear goals and pain points, and we came to a conclusion that we must perfect the navigation and chagrin logistics, so that it is sound-proof, and works for the user, not against them. 

## Beyond HCI
I believe the most valuable aspect of user research, not just for this project, but for everyday life, is the interview. It remains the best way to understand users on a personal, detailed level; there is a unique depth of insight you can only get from a one-on-one conversation. However, the true skill lies in the approach. You must build enough trust and comfort with the interviewee so they feel safe sharing their genuine pain points. Ultimately, the success of our entire research phase was built on the foundation of these honest conversations.
The ultimate goal of any business is to acquire and retain paying customers, it’s as simple as that. Mastering how to approach a user, uncover their core pain points, and offer a legitimate solution is a crucial skill set in sales, technology, and beyond. I believe the knowledge and skills I’ve obtained during this process are universally applicable; leveraging them in any future role will significantly improve my productivity and reliability. These techniques are essential whether you are building a startup from the ground up or A/B testing a new feature at a major tech company.
My biggest takeaway from this process is realizing the extent to which people rely on technology, and how significantly it can let them down if not designed correctly. While EVs represent the future of transportation, the supporting software has not kept pace with the shift in hardware, ultimately degrading the user experience. I’ve concluded that technology must evolve simultaneously; new hardware requires a parallel evolution in supportive software to ensure the transition is seamless and the experience is better for the user in every possible way.
