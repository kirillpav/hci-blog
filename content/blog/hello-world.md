## Who I am

My name is Kirill Pavlov, and I am currently a senior studying in Computer Science at San Francisco State University. I am originally from Ukraine, but I lived all over the world before moving to the US. My ultimate goal is to become a world-class software engineer. However, I don't just want to write code that works; I want to be a desired professional. Aside from work, I am passionate about sports, video games (CS2 mostly) and reading books.

## Excitements about HCI

For a long time, I've been fascinated with design, specifically the invisible "magic" that separates a tool from an experience. Why do Apple products feel so premium to use? It isn't just the brushed aluminum or the high-resolution displays. It's the way a haptic motor vibrates just enough to confirm an action, or how a menu bounces when you reach the end of a list.

In our modern age, HCI is the most important aspect of the digital world. Our cars, our phones, even our air fryers are interfaces. Everything around us was designed with a specific intent and a specific audience in mind. I am excited to dive deeper into the psychological depths of these designs. I want to move beyond the "what" and "how" and learn about the "why." Why do I have such strong opinions about the apps I use? I hope to eventually learn how to craft experiences that make other users feel that same sense of delight and premium quality.

## Examples of HCI in my life

The realization that HCI impacts my quality of life came through a simple switch in music streaming app. For years, I was a Spotify user. In high school, I didn't care much about aesthetics and as long as the music played, I was happy. However, as my appreciation for design grew, I found myself annoyed by Spotify's UI. It felt cluttered, algorithmic and wrong.

I tried Apple Music, and immediately felt different. It wasn't just about the sound, it was the experience and the focus on the album art. My experience with the app changed my experience with the music. After this, I began changing my weather apps, my navigation tools, my workout trackers, and everything else I used.

This was a turning point in my development career. I stopped looking at software as a set of functional requirements and started looking at it as an experience.

## Growth with HCI

There is a specific role that I want to be: Design Engineer. In the traditional tech role, there is often a wall between the designer and the engineer. The Design Engineer combines these two. I love the act of building, the logic, the data structures, the performance optimization, but I also have strong opinions about design.

I want to build things that feel "premium". By studying the way humans perceive information and react to software, I can bring care and meaning to my code. I want to be the engineer who builds for the user, ensuring that the final product isn't just a functional piece of logic, but something that the user feels good about using.

## Our Project

We are building an AI powered road trip assistant for EV drivers. I love road trips; something about being on the road, the freedom to go wherever you please, it is just a magical feeling. Reading old books like On The Road, trips pre technology have a charm to them, this note of mystery and excitement. Unfortunately, this is not the case today. There's so much planning, so many pins on google maps, that it becomes overwhelming, especially going to places you've never been before. With our project, I hope to simplify that. I've already used AI for planning trips, and it has been a mediocre experience, because these apps are not built for it, but they have the opportunity to be if they are fine tuned. I believe that a project like ours would be great for all road trippers. By the end of the project I hope to have a fully designed and thought out project, and afterwards to attempt to build it into a real functioning application!
